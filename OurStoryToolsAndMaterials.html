Tools and Materials

“Craftsmen use simple wooden looms, locally made bobbins, and traditional dyeing vessels. Cotton yarns and natural dyes like turmeric and indigo once dominated, though synthetic options are now more common.”

Tools and Materials

Used to making Dhalapathar

The unique and intricate weaving techniques of Dhalapathar textiles do not rely on modern tools like jacquard, which would require 400-600 hooks. Instead, Dhalapathar weavers use a traditional method involving wooden sticks called 'Chiari.' This rare technique includes a process known as shedding and picking of an extra weft.

Chadki

This age-old wooden tool spins raw fibers like cotton or silk into yarn. Basically it helps to fining the yarn.

Jhada

Jhada is a frame like structure in which all the bobbins are put to make the wrapping wheel.

Manku

Manku (Shuttle) is a wooden instrument, used in weaving to carry the weft thread back and forth between the warp threads.

Natei

Natei refers to the warp yarn the lengthwise threads that create the fabric's foundation. During the warping stage, natei is carefully wound onto the loom, setting the stage for the intricate weaving process.

Aaratha

In handloom weaving, aaratha (warping) is a crucial step.It involves preparing warp threads, aligning them correctly, and creating patterns.

Baw

Baw is the part of the frame , which have many circular eyes to separate each designed threads.

Nali

Nali is a small spool (pirn), made up of wood, used to wind the prepared yarn for dyeing or warping, called bobbin.

Dhada

Dhada is the part of the tie & dye process in which the weft threads are used to color.

Compashi

Compashi is a wooden machine which is used to stretch the weaved clothes from the unweaven threads during weaving technique.

Frame

This frame helps arrange the warp yarns (lengthwise threads) in a specific order on the loom.
